## §1.1 From Coherence Frames to Joint Geometry

Paper 1 introduced coherence relativity: a geometric framework in which quantum measurement is a change of coherence frame, not a dynamical collapse. The Born rule emerges as the unique probability measure invariant under coherence-frame transformations (A1–A4), resolving the measurement problem without modifying unitary dynamics, postulating many worlds, or retreating to subjectivism.

Paper 1 established the geometry of $\Sigma$ alone — the manifold of coherence frames at a fixed spacetime point. This paper extends the formalism to the joint manifold $M \times \Sigma$, where $M$ is spacetime. The extension is forced by physics: decoherence conditions vary across spacetime, and the coherence frame that is natural in one environment may be inappropriate in another.

## §1.2 What This Paper Delivers

Seven open questions from Paper 1 are addressed: the cross-term tensor $T_{M\Sigma}$ coupling spacetime to coherence evolution (§2.1); the $\delta\lambda$ formalism controlling the quantum-classical transition (§2.2); the Markov criterion for classicalization (§2.6); the mixed-state Born rule via purification (§2.4); equations of motion on $M \times \Sigma$ with the frame-lag mechanism (§4); $C^1$ regularity from derived compactification (§4.5); and a holographic structure conjecture (§5).

The central structural result is derived compactification. The coherence-frame axioms, applied to the qubit, uniquely produce $S^2$ as the coherence manifold. The Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^3 \to S^2$ emerges as the minimal ($c_1 = 1$) $U(1)$ bundle — a topological consequence, not a postulate. The string landscape of $\sim 10^{500}$ Calabi-Yau topologies collapses to a single geometric outcome: a bounded interval $r \in [0, r_{\max}]$ with warp factor $A(r) = \cos(\sqrt{2}\,r)$, where $r_{\max} = \pi/(2\sqrt{2})$ is topologically frozen by $k^2 = 2$ from the $\mathbb{CP}^1$ Fubini-Study eigenvalue.

## §1.3 The KCR-Cone as Worked Example

To test the framework, we evaluate it on the Kaluza–Coherence Relativity Cone (KCR-Cone) — named to distinguish it from Klein's 1926 compactification, which is unnecessary here. The KCR-Cone is a 5D warped geometry with metric $ds^2 = A^2(r)\,\eta_{\mu\nu}\,dx^\mu dx^\nu + dr^2$, genuinely five-dimensional. No Klein circle appears; charge quantization comes from the Berry phase ($c_1 = 1$ on $\mathbb{CP}^1$), and the massive mode spectrum arises from a volcano potential on the derived interval — giving non-linear KCR mode spacing $m_n/m_1 \approx 1, 1.67, 2.32, 2.97$ that distinguishes derived compactification from Klein's linear $1, 2, 3, 4$.

The self-consistency analysis yields three results:

**SC3 — Cosmological constant.** The Casimir energy on the derived interval with Dirichlet boundary conditions gives $L^* \in [56, 69]\,\mu\mathrm{m}$, passing the inverse-square-law bound (Lee et al. 2020, 44 μm) with 3× margin. The Atiyah-Singer index theorem constrains the mode count topologically via $c_1 = 1$ (Level 1b correction < 1%). Stabilization of $L^*$ requires flux quantization (deferred to Paper III).

## §1.4 Posture

The KCR-Cone is not declared the answer. It is the first worked example of a framework whose content is testable: the non-linear KCR mode spacing, the ISL prediction at $L^* \sim 60\,\mu\mathrm{m}$, null dark-matter detection, $\alpha = 3/2$ as an exact geometric prediction, and the pointer-basis dependence of the Zeno/anti-Zeno crossover (Paper 1, Prediction 7) are all falsifiable. Other geometries — higher Chern classes, non-abelian fiber structures — can and should be tested against the same five-level hierarchy.
