## What Is Rei-ho?

Rei-ho is a traditional Japanese sit-to-stand practice that uses slow, upright movements to strengthen the knees and thighs without weights or equipment. It looks simple, but it delivers powerful benefits for balance, mobility, and everyday strength, especially as you age.[1]

Rei-ho focuses on controlled squats and chair sit-to-stands where your torso stays upright, your feet stay together, and each repetition takes about four to five seconds. This style keeps your thigh muscles working continuously while reducing strain on your back and joints.[1]

***

## Brief Samurai History

Rei-ho comes from the daily movement patterns of Japan’s samurai era, when people routinely sat on the floor, slept on futons, and used squat toilets. These habits required frequent squatting and standing, naturally building strong legs and stable knees.[1]

As Western-style chairs and beds replaced traditional floor living, much of that natural strength training disappeared, and Rei-ho evolved as a way to preserve those beneficial movements in a structured practice. Modern research has now revisited Rei-ho and confirmed it can significantly increase knee strength in just a few minutes per day.[1]

## How to Perform Rei-ho (Step by Step)

Use this as your on-page instructional script and as a guide for a demo video.

1. **Starting position**  
   - Stand tall with your feet together or very close, toes pointing forward.[1]
   - Keep your chest lifted and your torso upright, looking straight ahead.[1]

2. **Controlled “samurai” squat**  
   - Begin to bend your knees slowly, keeping your trunk vertical instead of leaning forward.[1]
   - Let your knees travel forward while your hips sink down, but stop before your hips drop below knee level so your butt stays above your knees.[1]

3. **Timing and tempo**  
   - Count about four to five seconds as you lower: “1–2–3–4” down.[1]
   - Pause briefly at the bottom while staying balanced and upright, keeping tension in your thighs.[1]

4. **Returning to standing**  
   - Press through the balls of your feet and mid-foot, not just your heels, to stand back up in another four to five seconds.[1]
   - Avoid using your arms to push off your thighs or a chair; let your legs do the work as you rise.[1]

5. **Repetitions and session length**  
   - Aim for five minutes of these slow repetitions, about four days per week.[1]
   - In a clinical study, this schedule increased knee extension strength by about 25% over 12 weeks in adults who had never trained Rei-ho before.[1]

6. **Chair-assisted version (for beginners or seniors)**  
   - Sit toward the front of a sturdy chair with feet together and knees bent about 90 degrees.[1]
   - Cross your arms over your chest, lean only slightly forward if needed, and stand up over four to five seconds, then sit back down over four to five seconds, keeping control the whole way.[1]

Encourage visitors to start with a small number of slow repetitions and build up to a full five-minute block as their strength improves.[1]

## Why Rei-ho Can Be Better Than Regular Squats

Use this section to position Rei-ho as a superior option for many clients, especially older adults and those with joint concerns.

- **Higher quadriceps focus**  
  Traditional squats often involve leaning the torso forward, which shifts more load to the hips and back. Rei-ho keeps the torso upright and the knees driving forward, placing more emphasis on the quadriceps—the key muscles for standing, walking, and stair climbing.[1]

- **Greater strength gains with less time**  
  In research, a Rei-ho program of short daily sessions increased knee strength by about 25.9% over three months, while some squat programs with far more repetitions only achieved 8–16%. This makes Rei-ho a highly **efficient** way to build leg strength for busy people.[1]

- **Lower joint and cardiovascular stress**  
  The slow, controlled tempo reduces sudden forces on the knees, ligaments, and lower back, and helps keep blood pressure responses modest compared with high-intensity strength training. This makes Rei-ho especially suitable for older adults and those concerned about joint or heart strain.[1]

- **Bodyweight, equipment-free, and accessible**  
  Rei-ho uses only bodyweight, no gym and no machines, lowering barriers for beginners and those who feel intimidated by traditional strength training. Because it fits into five-minute windows, adherence is easier than with long, complex workout plans.[1]

## Who Especially Needs Rei-ho?

Here is copy you can use to speak directly to target groups.

- **Seniors wanting to stay independent**  
  Losing leg strength makes standing from a chair, getting out of a car, and climbing stairs more difficult and increases the risk of falls. Rei-ho directly trains the thigh muscles responsible for these movements, helping seniors maintain balance, confidence, and independence.[1]

- **People with knee concerns or fear of injury**  
  Many avoid squats because they worry about “bad knees” or past joint issues. The slow, partial-range Rei-ho pattern keeps the hips above the knees and removes the fast, jerky motion that often aggravates joints, offering a safer alternative for rebuilding strength.[1]

- **Busy professionals with limited time**  
  A five-minute routine, four times per week, has been shown to produce measurable strength gains, making it ideal for those who cannot commit to long gym sessions. Consistency, not duration, is the key, and Rei-ho is designed to be easy to stick with.[1]

- **Beginners and deconditioned clients**  
  For people who feel out of shape or intimidated by weights, Rei-ho provides a structured yet gentle way to reintroduce strength training. Starting with chair-assisted sit-to-stands allows gradual progression while avoiding overload.[1]
