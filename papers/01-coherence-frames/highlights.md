## Coherence Relativity I: Coherence Frames, Frame Invariance, and the Born Rule

**5 Key Points for Editors and Reviewers**

1. **Novel Geometric Framework**: Introduces coherence space Σ = U(d)/T^d as a physical manifold where quantum "collapse" is reinterpreted as transformation between coherence frames—analogous to how special relativity reinterprets simultaneity as frame-relative.

2. **Born Rule Derivation from First Principles**: Derives μ(ψ_i) = |c_i|² as the unique probability measure invariant under coherence-frame relabelings and splittings, unifying Gleason's theorem, Zurek's envariance, and operational approaches within a single geometric principle.

3. **Six Testable Experimental Predictions**: Distinguishes coherence relativity from standard quantum mechanics through measurable effects including frame-transformation hysteresis, non-linear visibility scaling, and transformation timescales (~10-100 μs in superconducting qubits).

4. **Resolution of Quantum Paradoxes**: Dissolves the measurement problem, wave function collapse, and quantum-classical boundary by showing these are frame-dependent descriptions rather than physical processes—no modification of unitary dynamics required.

5. **Information-Geometric Foundation**: Employs Fubini-Study metric on coherence space to quantify the "distance" between observational perspectives, providing a rigorous mathematical structure for quantum measurement theory with connections to thermodynamics and large deviation theory.

## Why This Matters

**For Quantum Foundations:**  
Offers a third way between collapse theories (which modify dynamics) and many-worlds (which proliferates ontology), maintaining both unitary evolution and single-world ontology through geometric reinterpretation.

**For Experimental Physics:**  
The most accessible predictions (hysteresis effects and transformation timescales) are testable with current technology in superconducting qubit and trapped ion experiments, potentially resolving century-old foundational questions empirically.

**For Mathematical Physics:**  
Connects quantum measurement theory to differential geometry, information geometry, and stochastic dynamics (Freidlin-Wentzell theory), opening new mathematical tools for foundational questions.
