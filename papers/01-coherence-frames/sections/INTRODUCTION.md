# Introduction

## The Measurement Problem as a Frame Problem

The quantum measurement problem—how definite outcomes emerge from superposed states—has persisted for nearly a century despite the extraordinary empirical success of quantum mechanics. At its core lies a conceptual asymmetry: the formalism describes systems evolving unitarily, yet observations yield definite results governed by the Born rule, p_k = |psi_k|^2. The standard resolution appends a measurement postulate that operates outside the unitary framework, creating what Penrose called "the most important open problem in physics" and what Bell termed "the shifty split" between quantum and classical domains.

Attempts to resolve this tension have proceeded along several distinct lines. Gleason's theorem (1957) showed the Born rule follows mathematically from non-contextuality on Hilbert space, but left the physical origin of non-contextuality unexplained. Zurek's envariance program (2003-2005) derived probabilities from entanglement symmetry, though critics note potential circularity in its assumptions. The Deutsch-Wallace decision-theoretic approach obtained Born weights from rationality axioms in an Everettian branching universe, at the cost of committing to many-worlds ontology. The decoherence program (Zeh, Zurek, Joos) explained the suppression of interference and the emergence of classical pointer states, but explicitly assumes the Born rule for reduced density matrices. Relational quantum mechanics (Rovelli) and QBism (Fuchs, Caves, Schack) reinterpret the formalism—making states observer-relative or subjective—without deriving the probability rule. Objective collapse models (GRW, CSL, Diosi-Penrose) modify the dynamics itself, introducing stochastic terms that force localization.

Each approach captures an essential aspect of the problem. Yet none provides simultaneously: (i) a derivation of the Born rule from physical principles, (ii) a geometric framework explaining *why* those principles hold, (iii) testable predictions distinguishing the framework from standard quantum mechanics, and (iv) ontological clarity about what exists independently of description.

## Coherence as Relativity

In this paper we propose that the measurement problem is not a dynamical problem requiring new physics, but a *frame problem* requiring a change in conceptual structure—analogous to the conceptual shift from Newtonian absolute time to the relativity of simultaneity in Einstein's special relativity.

The central idea is this: **coherence is frame-relative**. What appears as a definite, classical outcome in one coherence frame appears as a coherent superposition in another, just as events simultaneous in one inertial frame are non-simultaneous in another. There is no absolute fact about whether a system "has collapsed"; there are only frame-dependent descriptions of a single, frame-invariant physical reality.

We formalize this through four elements:

1. **Coherence space Sigma**: A manifold whose points represent coherence frames—distinct observational perspectives on quantum reality. Physical reality is described on a joint manifold M x Sigma, where M is emergent spacetime.

2. **Frame transformations**: Maps U_{F->F'} between coherence frames, analogous to Lorentz transformations, that describe how descriptions change when the measurement context changes. What we call "collapse" is not dynamical evolution but a frame transformation induced by an interaction that redefines which degrees of freedom encode definiteness.

3. **Born rule as frame-invariant measure**: We show that requiring a probability measure mu to be (a) invariant under all allowed coherence-frame transformations, (b) additive on orthogonal alternatives, and (c) continuous in the state coefficients uniquely fixes mu(psi_i) proportional to |c_i|^2. The Born rule thus emerges as the unique frame-invariant weight assignment—the "invariant interval" of coherence geometry.

4. **Tensor field T_AB**: A field on M x Sigma encoding the information geometry of coherence frames and the dynamics of frame transformations. In particular regimes, functionals derived from T reduce to quasipotential landscapes governing the metastability of classical configurations.

## Results and Organization

The derivation proceeds in four steps (Section 3). First, frame invariance under permutation and phase symmetry forces equal weights for equal-amplitude states. Second, frame-splitting constructions extend this to rational amplitude ratios. Third, continuity extends to arbitrary amplitudes, yielding the Born rule as the unique frame-invariant measure. Fourth, we show how the framework reduces to standard quantum mechanics in appropriate limits while predicting deviations in regimes involving frame-transformation dynamics, coherence-space curvature, and gravitational coupling.

We demonstrate the framework with a detailed worked example: the double-slit experiment analyzed in two coherence frames (Section 4). The interference frame F_int and the which-path frame F_wp describe the same physical reality from different perspectives, with the Born measure invariant across both. Bohr's complementarity emerges naturally as the statement that wave and particle descriptions correspond to different coherence frames.

We identify six testable predictions (Section 5) that distinguish coherence relativity from standard quantum mechanics and competing interpretations: non-linear visibility scaling in tunable interferometry, frame-transformation hysteresis in quantum erasure, coherence-space curvature effects in many-body systems, modified gravitational decoherence, measurable frame-transformation timescales via weak measurements, and coherence revival in closed systems.

Section 2 provides the mathematical framework—definitions, axioms, and the tensor field structure. Section 6 discusses implications including the resolution of the measurement problem, the relationship to quantum Darwinism and the emergence of objectivity, and comparisons with existing approaches. Section 7 summarizes and outlines future directions.
